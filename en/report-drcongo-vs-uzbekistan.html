📊 Post-Match Review · Tactics & Data · Full time DR Congo 3-1 Uzbekistan (HT 0-1) · 06-27

① How the Score Unfolded

This was a stunning comeback for the DRC history books. Uzbekistan struck first in the 10th minute as Eldor Shomurodov lobbed the ball over keeper Mpasi, leading 0-1 at the break. But DR Congo turned it around with three goals in the second half: Wissa won and converted a penalty in the 68th minute to level; Fiston Mayele completed the turnaround in the 78th (his finish came off a deflection from a Meschack Elia shot); and in stoppage time Wissa struck again from the edge of the box in the 90+1' for his brace, sealing it 3-1. The win sends DR Congo into the World Cup knockout stage for the first time in their history (as a best-third-placed side) — 52 years after their debut in 1974 (then as Zaire). Uzbekistan conceded three in the final 22 minutes and bow out, but their debut World Cup campaign was a creditable one.

③ Tactical Review

Desabre dropped the back five for 4-4-2 — "freeing the attack" was the tactical root of the comeback (transferable)
The preview flagged as the No.1 watch "can DRC free themselves from two games of 'lock-it-down' football and genuinely push up to attack". Desabre dropping the first two games' back five for a 4-4-2 is exactly that variable delivering — 19 shots, 5 on target, 53% possession, DRC finally turned defence into attack and were rewarded with a three-goal comeback. This directly validates the pre-match read: DRC's ceiling lies in "daring to push up".
Wissa + the front line firing — base case "Wissa/Bakambu firing up front" was directionally right
The base-case script named "Wissa + Bakambu firing up front". Wissa's brace (including the penalty) made him the decisive man, and with Mayele's go-ahead goal, DRC's attack finally clicked. It shows that when DRC are let off the leash, their front-line individual quality is enough to break a game open — their core asset for advancing.
Uzbekistan "scored first then collapsed" — defensive stamina and second-half management are the soft spot
Uzbekistan struck in the 10th minute and led at the break, only to ship three in the final 22 minutes. Their backline's stamina and organisation broke down after a long spell under pressure — leading on just 3 shots / 1 on target was a "low-quality lead" that was always hard to sustain. For their debut World Cup, a valuable but harsh lesson.
Keeper Mpasi conceded early — his tournament "clean-sheet" run ended, a reminder the defence is not impregnable
Mpasi had been error-free across two games this tournament but was beaten by Shomurodov's lob in the 10th minute. It shows DRC's earlier clean sheets leaned heavily on the dense protection of the back five; once they switched to a back four and pushed up, the defence exposed space. Against a strong attack like England in the knockouts, this "attack-defence balance" trade-off will be the key question.

📋 Quick Summary (Read This First)

This is the Group K final round's "third-place fight" — both teams are already out of the top two, but neither has been formally eliminated. The arithmetic is dead simple: a draw eliminates both; as long as there is a winner, the victor sneaks into the "eight best third-placed teams" on 3 or 4 points (this tournament's format has 48 teams in 12 groups, and a third-placed team can still advance if its record is good enough). DR Congo (DRC): 1 point, GD -1 — a win takes them to 4 points, most likely good enough for a best-third spot; Uzbekistan: 0 points, GD -7 — even a win needs a big improvement in goal difference plus hoping other groups' third-placed teams do poorly. So: DRC are effectively the proactive side, while Uzbekistan need a big win (whether the 3 points of a one-goal win is enough to qualify is an unknown, dependent on other groups). Historical context: DRC have already completed their first-ever World Cup goal and first-ever point this tournament via Wissa's header (a full 52 years after their first appearance in 1974); Uzbekistan are likewise at their debut World Cup, with Fayzullaev's one goal creating their first World Cup goal in history. 🇨🇩 DRC · Sebastien Desabre
Back five / expected to switch to a back four · defence first + Wissa/Bakambu finishing
  • Desabre used a back five (5-3-2) in both games this tournament: a solid defence to withstand stronger sides, relying on individual quality and counters. It worked extremely well against Portugal (just 1 conceded, generating an xG of 0.82), but the attack all but vanished against Colombia (xG 0.37, a 0-1 loss).
  • Cross-reference: DRC have shown the ability to attack with twin strikers + wing-backs at the likes of the 2023 AFCON, but Desabre has been especially conservative at the World Cup. The "must win" here means the tactics have to evolve — Sports Mole predicts he will finally let loose (Kapuadi out → a back four freeing up attacking resources).
  • This match's entry point: if they switch to a back four, Wan-Bissaka (right) + Masuaku (left) provide much more width on the two flanks, with more space for Bongonda, Wissa and Bakambu. Uzbekistan's defence (5 conceded against Portugal) is prone to getting out of position under pressure — DRC should aggressively use the flanks and depth. The key is whether Desabre dares to genuinely change, rather than talk about changing tactics while still actually waiting on the opponent.
🇺🇿 Uzbekistan · Fabio Cannavaro
3-4-2-1 / 4-4-2 · Shomurodov the finishing focal point + Fayzullaev the creative core
  • Cannavaro leads Uzbekistan with a 3-4-2-1 as the main framework, Shomurodov a lone-striker focal point, Fayzullaev + Urunov as twin 10s, four midfielders controlling the centre. They were competitive early against Colombia (1-3) (Fayzullaev scoring the equaliser), but completely collapsed against Portugal (0-5).
  • Cross-reference: Uzbekistan lost to Canada (0-2) and the Netherlands (0-2) in friendlies, with a clear overall gap to top sides; they are only competitive against same-tier opponents (DRC are exactly that tier). Khusanov's defensive quality is the strongest in the back line, but the defence is over-reliant on him overall and prone to errors.
  • This match's entry point: if DRC switch to a back four and push up proactively, Uzbekistan's counter-attacking space actually widens — if Shomurodov's pace and physicality find a rhythm, they can threaten DRC's defence. Fayzullaev is the most crucial individual: if he is man-marked out by DRC's midfield, Uzbekistan lose their attacking organiser and the game loses all competitiveness; if he performs, Uzbekistan have the confidence to chase an upset.
