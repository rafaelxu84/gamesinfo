📊 Post-Match Review · Tactics & Data · Full-time Jordan 1-3 Argentina (HT 0-2) · 06-27

① Score progression

With top spot already locked up, Argentina rotated heavily — Scaloni left Messi, Mac Allister and De Paul all on the bench, lining up in a 3-4-1-2 with an Otamendi-Senesi-Tagliafico back three, Lautaro alongside Julián Álvarez up top and Lo Celso at the tip of midfield. Even as a second-string XI, Argentina controlled the game: Lo Celso curled in a free-kick from the edge of the box on 19 minutes, and Lautaro converted a penalty on 31 minutes for a 2-0 half-time lead. In the second half, substitute Mousa Al-Tamari pulled one back for Jordan with a fine team move, sending the World Cup debutants out with a goal to their name; then Messi made it 3-1 around the 80th minute with a trademark free-kick that crept into the bottom corner. With it, Messi became the first man ever to score in seven consecutive World Cup matches; Lautaro's penalty was Argentina's first non-Messi World Cup penalty converted since 2002. Argentina won Group J with a perfect three wins, 9 points and the best goal difference; Jordan lost all three and exit, but with honour.

③ Tactical review

"Argentina's heavy rotation + Messi's status" was the top pre-match variable — it landed as Messi benched, 9 changes
The preview flagged "top spot settled, Scaloni will rotate, Messi may be benched." The result: Messi, Mac Allister and De Paul all benched, a switch to 3-4-1-2. It shows this Argentina has the squad depth to rotate heavily at champion-level intensity without dropping off — real reassurance for knockout-stage fitness management.
Set pieces were the second string's main breakthrough — Lo Celso and Messi free-kicks
Two direct free-kicks (Lo Celso 19', Messi ~80') plus a penalty produced all three goals. It shows that when open-play creativity dips slightly due to rotation, Argentina's set-piece quality remains a top-tier weapon — a transferable conclusion: against packed minnow defences, Argentina's dead-ball threat is often their most reliable source of goals.
Penalty duties spread — Lautaro converts, easing the single-point reliance on Messi
Lautaro netted Argentina's first non-Messi World Cup penalty since 2002. It shows the team is actively de-Messi-ing spot-kick responsibility; in a knockout shootout, having multiple reliable takers is a hidden edge.
Jordan out with honour — Al-Tamari a genuine highlight, but finishing and defensive depth fall short
Substitute Al-Tamari scored a fine team goal, the high point of Jordan's debut World Cup. But three defeats and the worst goal difference show Jordan's floor — defensive resilience and finishing efficiency against top sides — is still the main gap to world-class teams; the path forward lies in defensive organisation and wide finishing.

PPDA: passes allowed per defensive action; the lower the value, the more aggressive the press; Jordan's low block means an extremely high PPDA.
Field tilt: share of final-third touches; measures territorial tilt; expected to tilt entirely toward Argentina in this game.

4 Tactical Styles & Head Coaches

Every conclusion cross-references both teams' actual approaches and results across their last two major tournaments (Argentina: 2022 World Cup champions + 2021 Copa América; Jordan: 2023/2024 Asian Cups + two rounds of this World Cup)
🇯🇴 Jordan · Jamal Sellami
3-4-2-1 / 5-2-3 (when defending) · an Al-Tamari-centred counter-attacking system
  • Sellami (Moroccan, took over in 2024) has built Jordan into a compact low block mimicking Morocco 2022: 3 centre-backs, wing-backs providing width, becoming a 5-2-3 low block out of possession, then transitioning quickly and using Al-Tamari's wide pace to open space. It is precisely this system that took Jordan through the Asian qualifying zone.
  • Cross-reference: Jordan reached the final of the 2023 Asian Cup (0-1 to Qatar) and went out in the Round of 16 at the 2024 Asian Cup; the system is mature enough to compete with strong Asian sides, but its attacking power is extremely limited against top European / South American teams (a first this tournament). Against Algeria they led but the collapse in corner defence was a signature problem.
  • This match's response: Jordan will defend with everything, waiting for mistakes from a rotated Argentina or Al-Tamari's counter-attacking chances. The probability of scoring is low, but not zero — they scored in both games, which shows that given enough time any opponent can be breached. If a rotated Argentina's possession tempo drops, Jordan's low-block resistance has some space.
🇦🇷 Argentina · Lionel Scaloni
4-3-3 / 4-4-2 · rotated XI · focused on R32 preparation
  • Scaloni's Argentina are the 2022 World Cup champions + 2021 Copa América champions, with a mature system and extremely deep squad. Heavy rotation is expected here: rotation players such as Musso/Molina/Senesi/Lo Celso/Paredes/V. Barco/Nico Paz/Nico González all have international experience, more than enough against Jordan.
  • Cross-reference: at the 2022 World Cup Argentina, with a not-fully-rotated side in the group stage, still beat Poland 2-0 and advanced; this tournament they kept clean sheets against both Algeria (first-choice 3-0) and Austria (first-choice 2-0), showing an extremely high floor. Even a "bench version" poses a threat to Jordan far beyond Jordan's defensive capacity.
  • This match's response: possession + wide penetration (the pace of Nico Paz/Nico González) is the main thread; Julián Álvarez if he starts will be the centre-forward focal point; the possession tempo will dip slightly relative to the first-choice version but will still dominate throughout. Key unknown: how long Messi plays — if he plays, even 45 minutes, both the goal count and handicap depth will shift upward.
