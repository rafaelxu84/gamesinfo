① Scoring Sequence

Colombia edged DR Congo 1-0 thanks to a Daniel Muñoz goal in the 76th minute, sealing a knockout-stage berth ahead of schedule (6 pts). The Crystal Palace defender pounced on an opposition defensive lapse to score, after Colombia had been bottled up for a long stretch by their opponents' resolute defending. DR Congo keeper Lionel Mpasi made 8 saves to keep the score down, while Colombia keeper Vargas had almost nothing to do (just 1 save). James Rodríguez created 5 chances across the match — the first Colombian to create 5 chances in a single World Cup game since Valderrama against England in 1998.

③ Deep-metric quick reference (what these "xG-like" metrics each mean)

xG / npxG: total quality of shooting chances; stripping penalties better reflects open-play creativity.
xGA / xGC: quality of chances opponents create against you, measuring true defensive level.
xGD / 90: xG−xGA, the single best proxy for overall strength.
xG per shot: quality per shot — low value = lots of long shots / poor chances.
PPDA: passes allowed per defensive action; the lower the value, the more aggressive the press.
Field tilt: share of final-third touches, measuring territorial tilt rather than mere possession.

4 Tactical Styles & Head Coaches

Every conclusion cross-references both teams' actual play and results across their last two major tournaments (Colombia: Copa América 2024 runners-up / 2021 third place; DR Congo: AFCON 2023 fourth / AFCON 2025 last 16)
🇨🇴 Colombia · Néstor Lorenzo
4-2-3-1 possession press + James playmaking, Díaz the wing spark
  • Cross-reference of last two major tournaments: at Copa América 2024 (runners-up) Colombia built around James as the midfield brain, high-intensity pressing and wing (Díaz) attacking, going long unbeaten before the final; Copa América 2021 (third place) likewise leaned on individual quality in the middle and final third and counter-attacking efficiency — their tournament maturity and individual ceiling clearly lead DR Congo.
  • Cross-reference this tournament's MD1: a 3-1 win over Uzbekistan, but bottled up by a low block for 40 minutes and even pegged back to level — confirming that "a possession advantage does not automatically convert into goals," and that Díaz/James-level brilliance is needed to crack the block. ESPN believes Lorenzo should platform Díaz more thoroughly.
  • This match's entry point: DR Congo are a more mature block than Uzbekistan (they bottled Portugal into zero breakthrough), so Colombia must break through early via Díaz's wing spark + set pieces (the MD1 stoppage-time winner came from a crossed header) to avoid another slow start; meanwhile they must guard against Wissa/Bakambu counters in behind after pushing up.
🇨🇩 DR Congo · Sébastien Desabre
5-3-2 / low block + fast counters and set pieces
  • Cross-reference of last two major tournaments: at AFCON 2023 they knocked out Egypt on penalties and finished fourth — proving Desabre's counter system can limit strong sides in the knockouts and drag games into low scores; at AFCON 2025 they advanced unbeaten (1-0 over Benin, 1-1 with Senegal, 3-0 over Botswana), able to hold draws against strong sides.
  • Cross-reference this tournament's MD1: a 5-3-2 kept Portugal outside the box (Portugal 80% possession, zero breakthrough), 25% possession yet 8 shots and out-xG, with Wissa's single-point pace scoring the nation's first goal — this script is just as viable against a pushed-up Colombia, and ESPN even suggests they could more proactively exploit the space behind Colombia.
  • This match's response: a three/five-man back line (led by Mbemba) compresses the space for Díaz/Arias's wing crosses and dribbles, seeking points via Wissa's pace on the break and Bakambu's hold-up, with set pieces a supplementary route. The risk is that Colombia's individual ability is more direct and incisive than Portugal's, and there is no altitude drag — if concentration drops, the goals conceded could be amplified. But their AFCON record suggests they are unlikely to be routed.
