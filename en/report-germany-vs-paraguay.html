📊 Post-Match Review: Tactics & Data · FT Germany 1-1 Paraguay (pens 3-4, Paraguay advance) · HT 0-1 · 06-29

① Match progression (incl. shootout)

Gillette Stadium, Foxborough, Boston: a world-class upset in which Germany dominated throughout yet were knocked out on penalties. Germany had 76% possession and overwhelming passing numbers but could never turn that control into enough goals; Paraguay executed their pre-match script of a deep, packed block plus counters and ground the game down. On 42 minutes, Julio Enciso headed in a Galarza cross to put Paraguay 1-0 up at the break; on 54 minutes Kai Havertz headed home a Wirtz delivery to level it at 1-1. Germany then had a Tah goal from a corner ruled out by VAR for a foul on the keeper (Gill), and with no further goal in 90 or 120 minutes it went to penalties. Paraguay won the shootout 4-3: Germany missed three of their kicks — Havertz (saved by Gill), Woltemade (saved by Gill) and Tah (blazed over the bar); Paraguay missed two as Sanabria failed to score and Balbuena's would-be winner was saved by Neuer; in sudden death Tah fired over and Jose Canale converted to seal the win. It was the first time Germany have ever lost a World Cup penalty shootout, and a second straight tournament failing to reach the Last 16 (group exit in 2022, R32 exit now); Paraguay reach the World Cup Round of 16 for the first time since 2010. Multiple outlets called it one of the biggest knockout-stage upsets in World Cup history.

③ Tactical review

"Germany firepower vs Paraguay's block" scenario delivered — but the block won
The pre-match read had Germany as an overwhelming favourite (no-vig ≈68%) with Paraguay disrupting via a disciplined block plus counters. That is exactly how it played: Germany had 76% possession and dominated the passing, but mustered only 0.72 xG and never sustained penetration of Paraguay's low block. Transferable conclusion: Germany's tiki-taka is pretty under Nagelsmann, but final-ball/finishing efficiency against a packed defence is a genuine weakness — high possession does not equal high threat.
Paraguay executed their disruption blueprint: deep block + one lethal cross/set-piece
Paraguay carried over the clean-sheet DNA from the Türkiye/Australia games, ceded 76% of the ball, pinned Germany in non-threatening areas, and killed them with a Galarza cross and an Enciso header. Transferable conclusion: against possession sides, "cede the ball + compress depth + punish from crosses/set pieces" is a repeatedly proven path — Paraguay have honed it to the limit, with the mental resilience to take it to penalties.
VAR voids Tah's corner goal: set-piece detail decides everything
A Germany corner goal was chalked off for a foul on the keeper; had it stood, Germany most likely advance early. Transferable conclusion: against a bus-parking side, set pieces are the most realistic way through — but under SAOT/VAR precision, physical contact in the box (especially on the keeper) carries almost no margin for error, and Germany paid for the detail.
The shootout: Germany's first-ever defeat, and even a Neuer save wasn't enough
Germany missed three (Havertz, Woltemade saved by Gill; Tah over the bar), and even Neuer's save of Balbuena's would-be winner couldn't rescue them. Transferable conclusion: cracks appeared in Germany's nerve under shootout pressure (a World Cup first); meanwhile keeper Gill's two saves were the X-factor — goalkeeping form is the decisive variable once a knockout reaches penalties.

4-2-3-1 · high possession + vertical penetration · Wirtz/Musiala/Havertz front three
  • Under Nagelsmann, Germany are built on high possession, vertical penetration and a high-quality front group. Wirtz and Musiala are the creative core, Havertz the focal-point finisher, Sané supplies wide pace, Kimmich links from right-back/midfield. The 9 goals in two rounds show the attacking ceiling.
  • Cross-reference: Germany exited the 2022 WC group, reached the Euro 2024 (hosts) quarter-final; here they were full of firepower over two rounds, but the 1-2 loss to Ecuador exposed focus and defensive issues when ahead/rotating. Centre-back depth leans on Rüdiger/Tah, and Schlotterbeck's absence is a concern.
  • This match: against Paraguay's deep, packed defence, how to patiently break the bus and avoid being hit on the break is the priority — exactly the scenario for Wirtz/Musiala's penetration and Havertz/Sané's finishing. Whether Nagelsmann restores his first-choice XI and repairs the final-day mindset is a watch point.
🇵🇾 Paraguay · Gustavo Alfaro
Deep defence + pragmatic counters, discipline first (has used a 5-4-1 to sit in)
  • Under Alfaro (an experienced South American coach), Paraguay are built on disciplined deep defending, collective space-compression and quick counters. After the opening 1-4 hammering by the USA they tightened up pragmatically, keeping back-to-back clean sheets — including holding a lead against Türkiye with 10 men. Galarza/Almirón provide the counters and creation.
  • Cross-reference: Paraguay reached the 2010 WC quarter-final (their best) and are known for defensive resilience; here they advanced as a best-third side with low attacking output (max 1 goal a game) but clinical counters. A textbook low-scoring, bus-parking side.
  • This match: against a comprehensively superior Germany, Paraguay will most likely keep the bus parked plus counters plus set-pieces, ceding the ball and looking for chances through Galarza/Almirón's counter pace and a defence led by Alderete. Holding Germany's firepower and exploiting their defensive frailty is their key to disruption.
