① Score progression

Argentina sealed qualification with an unhurried display: early on, VAR intervened to award Argentina a penalty (Lautaro fouled), and Messi's spot-kick was saved; but the maestro did not let the suspense linger long — in the 38th minute Messi met a low Facundo Medina cutback to slot home for 1-0; in the second half Argentina eased off and controlled the game, Austria never turning their territorial edge into shots on target, and in stoppage time 90+5' Messi added another to lock down 2-0. The two goals took Messi to 17 and 18 personal World Cup goals, making him the sole all-time men's World Cup top scorer (overtaking Klose's 16), and his 5th goal of this tournament. Argentina's two straight wins secured early qualification (all but confirming Group J top spot). Argentina were "never out of second gear" all match — they won without going full throttle.

③ Tactical review

"Messi dependency" literally delivered — but this time it was a strength
The pre-match thread flagged that "Argentina's attack relies too much on Messi; the second finisher Lautaro must step up." The result: both goals came from Messi (he also took the missed penalty), with dependency at 100%. But with the maestro in scorching form, that dependency became a certain asset rather than a risk. It shows that as long as Messi is on the pitch and hot, Argentina can beat mid-to-lower opponents without solving the "second outlet"; yet the moment they meet a strong side that can truly freeze Messi, the hidden danger of lacking a second finisher remains unanswered (Lautaro once again failed to score here).
Argentina 3 shots, 2 goals — won on efficiency, not dominance
Argentina had only 3 shots all match (2 on target, both scored), xG 1.05. It shows this "low-effort win" rode Messi's individual ceiling and ruthless finishing, not positional grinding — meet an opponent with stronger organisation that can stifle transitions in the knockouts, and this low-output attacking model magnifies the risk. With qualification already secured, the defending champions can afford to keep their cards hidden.
Rangnick's press neutralised by "low block + breakaway runs"; the set-piece weapon misfired
Austria progressed via 10 throw-ins and edged corners 2-0, yet had 0 shots on target and 0.24 xG. It shows that facing the press, Argentina actively dropped into a low block, using tackles (12) and second-ball protection to cut off Austria's transitions, leaving the press unable to manufacture shots; the "set pieces + veteran finishing" route most fancied for Austria pre-match never paid off, fully exposing the old problem of weak open-play firepower.
Austria's individual ball-carrying frozen (dribbles 2/11)
Austria completed just 2 of 11 dribbles (18%). It shows that against Argentina's compact low block, Austria could neither create mismatches with individual breaks nor supply penetrative passes — once the press stops working, this Rangnick side lacks a Plan B; in the final round against Algeria they need a way to break down a packed defence.

③ Deep-metric quick reference (what these "xG-like" metrics each represent)

xG / npxG: the total quality of shooting chances; with penalties removed it better reflects open-play creativity.
xGA / xGC: the quality of chances opponents create against you, measuring true defensive level.
xGD / 90: xG−xGA, the single best proxy for overall strength.
xG per shot: quality per shot — a low value = many long shots / poor chances.
PPDA: passes allowed per defensive action; the lower the value the more aggressive the press (Austria's low value).
Field tilt: share of final-third touches, measuring territorial tilt rather than pure possession.
