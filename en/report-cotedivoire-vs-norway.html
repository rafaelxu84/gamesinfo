① Score Timeline

At AT&T Stadium in Arlington, 69,661 fans watched a 2-1 knockout decided by a late Norway winner—the whole match was settled inside 90 minutes, with the outcome turning on one substitution and one stoppage-time save. Antonio Nusa opened the scoring for Norway on 39 minutes, assisted by captain Martin Ødegaard, sending Norway 1-0 up at the break. Côte d'Ivoire raised the tempo after halftime, and substitute Amad Diallo, on around the 60th minute, became the turning point—he not only produced a goal-line clearance at the back but also finished off a Nicolas Pépé assist on 74 minutes to level it at 1-1, briefly putting Côte d'Ivoire's first-ever knockout run on level terms. But the deadlock lasted only 12 more minutes—on 86 minutes Erling Haaland converted a Patrick Berg through ball to make it 2-1 and settle the tie. In stoppage time, Norway keeper Ørjan Nyland made a diving save to preserve the win; Norway progress 2-1 to face Brazil in the Round of 16, while Côte d'Ivoire exit at the first knockout round of their history.

③ Tactical Review

The "Norway control the game on individual ceiling" script delivered—but they won on efficiency, not domination
The pre-match read had Norway with the slight edge (no-vig ≈47%), with the base scenario being Norway controlling the game on the ceiling of Haaland and Ødegaard. That was borne out: Norway's 4-3-3 ceded possession (44%-56%) and territory (trailing 9-14 on shots, 3-14 on corners), yet won on superior chance quality (xG 2.02 vs 1.15, a 50% big-chance conversion rate). Transferable conclusion: Norway's knockout blueprint is "cede territory, not the result"—against a physical, pacey opponent, they willingly surrender the underlying stat sheet and rely on a handful of clinical finishes; this efficiency-first approach is repeatable in single-leg knockout football, but it also means that if their finishing dips, a statistically superior opponent could drag them into trouble.
Côte d'Ivoire's "volume over quality" crossing plan didn't translate into goals—finishing efficiency is the biggest structural gap in their first-ever knockout run
The pre-match storyline flagged Côte d'Ivoire banking on pacey wing transitions to engineer an upset. They did generate a volume edge (14 shots, 14 corners, 29 crosses, 48 box touches), but completed only 24% of crosses (7/29) and scored just 1 of 10 shots from inside the box. Transferable conclusion: Côte d'Ivoire's wide output already met or exceeded expectations—the issue isn't generating chances but the quality of the final ball and finish; against a European side with more physicality and better aerial defense (Norway won 58% of aerial duels), pure volume out wide is easy to defend against systematically.
Emerse Faé's bet on starting Pépé/Diomandé/Bonny was undone by a "late-arriving" impact substitute
The predicted lineup had Amad Diallo likely to start, but he instead came on as a substitute around the 60th minute—and immediately changed the course of the game (Sofascore's top rating of 9.4, a goal plus a goal-line clearance). Transferable conclusion: Côte d'Ivoire's starting XI didn't fully unlock their wing pace advantage—the game only really opened up once Diallo entered—suggesting Faé still has room to improve on tempo management and substitution timing in knockout football; against higher-intensity opponents, the most dangerous attacking weapon may need to start earlier.
The Berge + Ødegaard midfield pairing replicated their group-stage tempo control, avoiding the "over-reliance on Haaland" risk
The pre-match concern was that Norway needed to avoid concentrating too much attacking output in Haaland alone. In the event, Sander Berge and Martin Ødegaard combined for 144 passes attempted (129 completed), carrying the organizational load, and both the opening goal and the winner were directly created by midfield service (Ødegaard assisting Nusa, Berg assisting Haaland). Transferable conclusion: Norway did not bet everything on Haaland's individual quality—a stable midfield pairing kept generating chances, which is a more durable knockout asset than a single superstar; it means that even when Haaland is closely marked, Norway still have a second and third route to goal.
Kristoffer Ajer's "low-event, direct-clearance" defending blunted Côte d'Ivoire's counter-press
Data show Ajer completed 53 of 54 passes and made 13 clearances, rarely engaging in risky buildup out of the back and instead clearing danger directly to avoid turnovers under Côte d'Ivoire's press. Transferable conclusion: against a team like Côte d'Ivoire that thrives off winning the ball high and counter-pressing, Norway's defense chose the simplest, lowest-risk method to relieve pressure—a repeatable defensive philosophy against high-intensity pressing opponents, trading elegant buildup for minimized error risk.
